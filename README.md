## Outline

Many of the time series and econometric tools that have long been workhorses of empirical macroeconomic analysis at central banks were largely developed for an environment with comparatively few series and stable economic relationships. Two recent developments mean that these tools, while still useful, are no longer sufficient on their own: **Big Data** and **machine learning**. By Big Data we refer to the large and rapidly growing number of macroeconomic and financial time series available to researchers and policymakers. Where it was once common to build regressions or vector autoregressions (VAR) using only a handful of variables, it is now routine to specify models with dozens or even hundreds of variables. By machine learning we refer to the idea that, with appropriate models and software, computers can uncover relationships between variables even when those relationships are highly complex (e.g., due to a rapidly changing economic environment, financial crises, or the Covid-19 pandemic).

In macroeconomics, especially in the presence of Big Data, there is a real risk that any single class of model will not be rich enough empirically. This has motivated a growing interest in Bayesian and machine learning methods, often based on either parametric (possibly time-varying) models with adaptive shrinkage/regularisation or fully nonparametric models, which allow for nonlinear relations between dependent and explanatory variables without imposing a specific functional form, letting the data decide instead. In addition, in particular post-Covid, modelling techniques for handling heteroskedasticity, outliers, and risk are crucial in any applied macroeconometric toolkit nowadays.

This 3-day course provides a hands-on introduction to modern Bayesian macroeconometrics, with a particular emphasis on tools relevant to central bank research and policy analysis. The course comprises 11 hours of lectures delivered by Dr. Hauzenberger, combining relevant theoretical concepts with applications to real-world macroeconomic and financial datasets. All course materials will be made available via [GitHub](https://github.com/nhauzenb/modern-macroeconometrics), including associated **R** code for the empirical illustrations.
